Snapspot

+ case study + ui/ux + prototype
+ role: research, testing, design, prototype, ui
+ collaboration with Ashley Bass + Carol Newhard

SnapSpot was a prototype of a location scouting and social networking application geared towards photographers. The goal was to create an app that could compete with similar apps already on the market in terms of usability and functionality. We asked ourselves, how might we be able to empower photographers to minimize the time they spend scouting locations through a curated search engine and collective community of photographers?

research

competitor analysis

To create a prototype that would solve our how might we statement, we had to begin with looking at the current market for any similar applications and completing user research. To find out the similarities and differences between the current applications on the market, we created a list to compare and contrast a list of features from three that we discovered.

Out of the three applications, Explorest had the most features available and was the most versatile. While it was the most similar to what we wanted to create, the limitation of a free user made it difficult to access some information. Additionally, after comparing all three applications, we discovered a few problems:

  • Users have to have specific criteria in order to bring up locations. Rather than typing a generic location, there is no way for users to easily browse through locataions.
  • Functionality of integrated maps are limited through free or premium labels or do not exist.
  • Overall applications were not user-friendly (either too complex or too cluttered).
  • Metadata exists, but is not extensive enough for users to properly utilise.

user interviews

One of my roles was to conduct user interviews with photographers who identified as both hobbyists and professionals; I interviewed four individuals where half identified as each the former and the latter. Below is a portion of the list of questions I asked:

  • What level of photography would you classify yourself under?
  • Do you use any photography or image-related mobile applications?
  • How do you scout locations?

These were the top three questions that gave the most insight into their behaviors and patterns in terms of photography. From these interviews, it was important for me to note that professionals who shoot for magazines or editorials have no concern with scouting locations. On the contrary, freelancers and hobbyists often want or need knowledge of their current location.

key takeaways

  • Hobbyists and professional freelance photographers would find a location app more useful; having a wide array of locations to utilize for shoots is beneficial for them and their clients.
  • Most used apps mentioned by interviewees were Instagram, VSCO, and Lightroom, which are all photo-sharing or editing apps.
  • All interviewees prefer to use apps that are free and do not require payments to access extra features or settings.


ideate

personas

Following conducting user interviews, I created personas based on the list of key takeaways so I could gain further understanding of these photographers' perspectives. I listed out all the key takeaways from both user interviews and initial research and competitor analysis to create multiple personas. This helped us to empathize with our target audience and have the ability to focus on what key features we wanted to include in our prototype.

prototyping

user & app flow

Before drawing the initial user flow, we made a list of features we wanted to include via our initial research and comparison of existing applications on the market. To begin prototyping, I created a generic user and app flow to map out the main pages and features that we wanted that reflected our main priority: to create a location scouting and sharing application that also emphasized the aspect of a collective community.

lo-fidelity wireframes

To further visualize how the user flow would work on the screen, I created lo-fidelity wireframes to map out some of the main features from our list.


final design

branding & ui kit

A part of my role for the project was to create basic branding and develop a UI kit for the application. The logo consists of a basic camera silhouette and a location marker icon that mimics the action of a camera flash. To keep the application as minimalistic as possible, I settled on two sans-serif fonts and a small selection of icons.

hi-fidelity prototype

I created a hi-fidelity prototype in Adobe XD as a part of our deliverables as well as for usability testing. While creating the wireframes, standardized my design by implementing Apple's iOS UI Kit to keep it consistent and familiar.

usability testing

To conduct this test, I used the hi-fidelity prototype I created and allowed the users to explore the application. I used the same individuals from the interview process to test the final prototype. I asked users to make note of what they thought about the flow and if the app conveyed the list of initial ideas we had in mind for the final app.


key takeaways

positive feedback

  • The design of the app was familiar to other Apple iOS applications
  • Enjoyed the feature of having a map integrated within the application
  • Population of the app by users allows more variety

potential issues
  • Social media aspect of sharing and posting seems unnecessary (compared to Instagram or VSCO)
  • How would results populate if a user were to search a location on the map screen versus the search screen?
  • If the apps locations are submitted by users, what type of criteria would be needed to validate a submitted location?

solutions

Based on the feedback from usability testing, it seemed important to find a way to refine the social media aspect of the application. While many users stated that they wouldn't necessarily share and post photos if they had the option, they were interested in the possibility to share locations to users within the application. For the final prototype of the app, we decided to modify the social media aspect while still keeping the sense of community apparent. To simplify the social media aspect, we altered how photo posts would work for users. We limited the social interactions to only likes and removed the ability to comment on other people's posts. Additionally, the messaging function was changed to solely send locations.
